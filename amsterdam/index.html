Amsterdam is a small city that doesn't behave like one. Around 900,000 people live here — yet 20 million visitors arrive every year. The result is a city that somehow stays charming despite itself: canal houses tilting at precarious angles, cyclists who treat red lights as a suggestion, and world-class museums you can walk between in 15 minutes.

It's also the city of the Dutch Golden Age — a period when Amsterdam was the wealthiest place on earth, trading spices from Indonesia and building the canal ring that still defines the skyline today. That history is everywhere: in the Rijksmuseum's Rembrandts, in the narrow corridors of the Anne Frank House, in the houseboat moored outside your hotel window.

I've lived here since 2020. What follows is the guide I wish I'd had when I arrived — 52 attractions worth your time across 6 categories, a day-by-day itinerary, and honest booking advice you won't find on the official tourism sites. Including which queues actually move, and which don't.

How to Spend 3 Days in Amsterdam

Three days is the ideal first visit. Here's how to see the best of Amsterdam without rushing — structured around walking distance and opening times to minimise transit.

Day 1 Museums & Canals
Morning
Rijksmuseum — Arrive at 9am when it opens to beat the crowds. Focus on the Gallery of Honour for the Night Watch, then spend the rest of the morning exploring Dutch Golden Age works. Allow 2.5 hours minimum. The museum garden is free and worth 10 minutes of your time.
Afternoon
Van Gogh Museum — A 5-minute walk from the Rijksmuseum on Museumplein. Allow 1.5 hours for the permanent collection. Afterwards, cross Museumplein to the Stedelijk for an hour of modern and design art (or save for Day 2). Lunch at one of the cafés along Van Baerlestraat.
Evening
Canal Cruise — Book an evening canal cruise departing from near Leidseplein (10-minute walk from the museums). The city is beautiful lit up at dusk. Dinner in the Leidseplein area afterwards — Café de Spuyt or Brasserie Blazer are solid options.
Day 2 History, Jordaan & Hidden Gems
Morning
Anne Frank House — This must be pre-booked. Take the 9am slot if possible — emotionally it's better to visit with a clear head. Allow 2 hours. Afterwards, walk across the Westerkerk square and explore the beginning of the Jordaan neighbourhood.
Afternoon
Jordaan exploration — Wander the nine streets (de 9 Straatjes) for boutique shopping, then find lunch at a brown café. Walk south along the Prinsengracht canal to the Spui square and duck through the door to the Begijnhof (free, allow 30 minutes). Then head to the Bloemenmarkt on Singel canal — free to browse.
Evening
Heineken Experience — Book the late afternoon session (around 4pm). The experience includes two beers, so it's a natural start to your evening. Dinner at the Heineken neighbourhood (Pijp district) — De Pijp is Amsterdam's most food-rich neighbourhood with Albert Cuyp market stalls open until late.
Day 3 Noord, Skyline & Day Trip
Morning
A'DAM Lookout — Take the free GVB ferry behind Centraal Station to Amsterdam Noord (5-minute crossing). Head straight to the A'DAM tower for 360° views. If you dare, add the Over the Edge swing experience. The EYE Film Museum (free entry to the building) is a 5-minute walk along the waterfront.
Afternoon
NEMO Science Museum — Ferry back to Centraal and walk east along the IJ waterfront to NEMO. The rooftop terrace is free. If visiting with children, spend 2–3 hours inside; otherwise 1 hour on the roof with coffee from the café is a perfect way to spend a sunny afternoon.
Optional
Keukenhof day trip (spring only) — If visiting March 19 – May 10, swap the afternoon for a Keukenhof bus trip from Centraal Station (35 minutes). Pre-book combined bus + entry tickets. Peak tulip bloom is typically 2nd–3rd week of April.
💡 Booking order: Book Anne Frank House first (it sells out fastest), then Van Gogh Museum, then Keukenhof if visiting in spring. Everything else can be booked 24–48 hours ahead, though earlier is always better in July–August.

How to Get Around Amsterdam

Amsterdam is one of Europe's most navigable cities. Here's how locals — and smart tourists — get around:

🚲

Bike

The authentic Amsterdam way. Rent from €10/day at shops near Centraal Station. Stick to bike lanes and walk the bike in pedestrian zones.

🚋

Tram

The easiest public transport option. Lines 2, 11, 12, 19 cover most tourist areas. Use the OV-chipkaart or buy a day ticket (€9.50).

🚶

On Foot

The historic centre (Centrum) is very compact — most top attractions are within a 20-minute walk of each other. Wear comfortable shoes (cobblestones!).

⛵

Canal Boat

The hop-on hop-off canal bus stops near the Rijksmuseum, Anne Frank House, and Centraal Station. Great for a lazy museum day.

💡 Airport tip: Amsterdam Schiphol Airport has a direct train to Centraal Station every 10 minutes (17 mins, ~€5.40). Don't get a taxi — the train is faster and a fraction of the price.

A Brief History of Amsterdam

1275 — A Dam on the Amstel

Amsterdam's founding moment is unglamorous: a handful of fishermen built a dam across the Amstel river to control flooding. The settlement was called Amstelredamme. The Count of Holland granted it toll privileges in 1275 — the oldest written record of the city. Within a century it had a harbour, a market, and an ambition that the marshy ground beneath it couldn't contain.

The Golden Age — 1585 to 1672

Everything changed in 1585. The Spanish captured Antwerp — then Europe's richest trading port — and tens of thousands of Protestant merchants, skilled craftsmen, and Jewish traders fled north to Amsterdam. The city absorbed them all and turned their capital into the engine of something extraordinary.

In 1602, Amsterdam's merchants founded the VOC — the Dutch East India Company — the world's first publicly traded company. It had its own army, could wage war, negotiate treaties, and mint coins. Within decades, Dutch ships controlled the spice routes from the Moluccas to Manhattan. The wealth poured back into the city: the canal ring was dug between 1613 and 1665, 1,500 merchant houses were built along it, and painters like Rembrandt and Vermeer were kept busy recording the face of this new prosperity.

At its peak, Amsterdam was the wealthiest city on earth per capita. The Amsterdam Stock Exchange — the world's first — opened in 1602 on the Rokin. Tulip mania gripped the city in 1636–37, when a single Semper Augustus bulb sold for the price of a canal house. The canal ring was granted UNESCO World Heritage status in 2010, but it was conceived and built in a single extraordinary generation.

Decline, Occupation, and Resilience

The Rampjaar — Year of Disaster — arrived in 1672. France, England, and two German states attacked the Dutch Republic simultaneously. The Golden Age never fully recovered. By the 18th century, Amsterdam was living on inherited wealth. Napoleon occupied the city in 1795; his brother Louis Bonaparte briefly made it a capital of a puppet kingdom, sleeping in what is now the Royal Palace on Dam Square.

The Second World War left the deepest wound. The German occupation began in May 1940. Of the roughly 80,000 Jewish residents of Amsterdam, more than 75% were deported and murdered — one of the highest rates of Jewish loss in Western Europe. Anne Frank hid in a canal house on the Prinsengracht for 761 days before being betrayed in August 1944. She died in Bergen-Belsen in February 1945, three months before liberation. The house is now the city's most visited site — and the most sobering.

Amsterdam Today

Post-war Amsterdam rebuilt itself in waves. The 1960s brought the Provos — anarchist activists who turned the city into a laboratory for progressive urban politics, handing out free white bicycles and demanding the city belong to its residents. The squatter movement of the 1970s and 80s secured social housing that still exists. The city legalised same-sex marriage in 2001 — the first country in the world to do so — and hosts WorldPride in 2026.

Today 900,000 people live here across 96 nationalities. Around 20 million visitors arrive each year, drawn by the same things that made this city remarkable in the 17th century: the canals, the art, and the specific Dutch genius for making something extraordinary out of very flat, very wet ground.
