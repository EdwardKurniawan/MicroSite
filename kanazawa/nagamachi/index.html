Nagamachi

The samurai quarter — earthen walls, stone lanes, and the finest surviving samurai garden in Japan. A 10-minute walk from the castle that defined it.

About Nagamachi

Nagamachi (長町) was the residential district of the Maeda clan's upper and middle-ranking samurai retainers. It lies immediately southwest of Kanazawa Castle, positioned so that the samurai could defend the castle approaches — and so that the castle could keep an eye on them.

What survived is a network of earthen walls (dobei), narrow stone-paved lanes, and water channels (nagare) that once provided fire protection for the district. The walls are made from mud, straw, and lime plaster, covered with roof tiles — a technique that made them remarkably durable. In winter they are wrapped in straw matting (komo) to protect them from frost damage: an annual ritual that has been performed here for over three centuries.

The Nomura Samurai House (野村家) is the most important building in the district. It belonged to the Nomura family, who served the Maeda for seven generations. The house's interior garden — built around a koi pond, stone lanterns, and a 300-year-old maple tree — is considered one of the finest small gardens in Japan and was awarded the highest rating in a survey of Japanese gardens by a major international travel publication.

❄️
Winter visit (Dec–Feb): The dobei walls are wrapped in komo straw matting each November to protect them from frost. This gives Nagamachi a dramatically different winter appearance — the warm brown-gold straw against grey stone is one of Kanazawa's most distinctive seasonal sights.

Top Things to Do in Nagamachi

Free · Always open

1 Walk the Dobei Earthen Walls

The most atmospheric part of Nagamachi is the 10-minute loop through the main earthen-walled lanes (Nagamachi 2-chome). The walls rise two to three metres on either side of the narrow stone-paved lanes, creating a sense of enclosure that separates you completely from the modern city beyond. Early morning or late afternoon, with slanting light on the ochre walls, is when it's most beautiful. Free, always open, and rarely crowded outside peak tourist weekends.

¥550 · Daily 8:30am–5:30pm (Oct–Mar until 5pm)

2 Nomura Samurai House

Built in the late Edo period and relocated to its current site in the 1880s, the Nomura House is the finest accessible samurai residence in Kanazawa. The ground floor has a remarkable shoin-style reception room with carved transom windows, a coffered ceiling, and a tokonoma alcove containing a hanging scroll and an ikebana arrangement. Upstairs, the veranda overlooks the garden: a koi pond, carefully placed stone lanterns, a water basin carved from a single rock, and a 300-year-old maple that turns gold in November. Allow 40–60 minutes.

Free entry

3 Ashigaru-za Samurai Houses

Two smaller restored ashigaru (foot soldier) residences showing how lower-ranking samurai actually lived — closer to comfortable artisan households than warrior fortresses. The contrast with the Nomura House is informative: the hierarchy of Edo-period samurai society was replicated precisely in the scale and finish of their homes. Both buildings have free entry and are staffed by volunteers who can answer questions in basic English.

Free · Koi visible year-round

4 Water Channel Network

The narrow channels (nagare) running through the district are part of a water management system built in the Edo period. Koi swim through some of them. The channels were designed to enable rapid water access in case of fire — a constant threat in a district of wooden buildings. Follow them north from the Nomura House toward the Korinbo intersection and back through the narrower back lanes for a 20-minute circuit that most visitors miss entirely.

From ¥1,500 · 30–60 min sessions

5 Kaga Yuzen Silk Dyeing Workshop

Several workshops near Nagamachi offer hands-on Kaga Yuzen (traditional silk dyeing) experiences, from ¥1,500 for a 30-minute session. The tradition dates from the 1600s and is one of Kanazawa's designated UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritages. The dyeing technique — characterised by delicate botanical motifs with deliberately faded outer edges — was developed specifically for the Maeda court. Most workshops offer simple handkerchief or fan dyeing in under an hour; no booking needed for walk-in sessions.

Why Nagamachi survived intact: Unlike Kyoto, Kanazawa was never bombed during World War Two — it had no military industry. And unlike many Japanese cities, the samurai district was never cleared for postwar development because the earthen walls, water channels, and stone lanes were designated protected heritage sites in the 1970s. What you walk through today is genuinely Edo-period urban fabric, not a reconstruction.

Getting to Nagamachi

🚌

Loop Bus

Korinbo stop — 5-minute walk to the district entrance. ¥200 per ride or ¥600 day pass. Runs every 15–20 minutes from Kanazawa Station.

🏯

From Kenroku-en

15-minute walk southwest from Kenroku-en's Katamachi gate. The most natural sequence — garden, castle, samurai district — all on foot.

🎨

From 21st Century Museum

10-minute walk north. Excellent pairing: contemporary art then feudal Japan, five minutes apart. Loop back via Katamachi for dinner.

Best combined with:

🎨 21st Century Museum — 5 min walk
🍜 Katamachi evening dining — 10 min walk
🏯 Kanazawa Castle — 15 min walk
Timing tip: Nagamachi is compact enough that an hour is sufficient for a focused visit. Combine it with the 21st Century Museum in the same afternoon — enter the museum at 10am when it opens, walk to Nagamachi by 1pm, then return to Katamachi for an early dinner. This is the most efficient half-day circuit in Kanazawa.

Where to Eat & Drink Near Nagamachi

Kaga Yuzen Workshop Cafe
Light Lunch & Sweets ¥

Light lunch inside a working silk dyeing studio — set menus with seasonal side dishes, served in a quietly beautiful converted machiya. The most atmospheric lunch stop in the district.

Miyoshiya
Wagashi ¥

Traditional wagashi (Japanese confectionery) shop with a Nagamachi branch. The wagashi are made daily and change with the seasons — spring brings cherry blossom shapes, autumn produces persimmon and chestnut varieties. Take-away or eat at the small counter with matcha.

Gyoza no Ohsho
Chinese-style Gyoza ¥

Casual gyoza dumpling restaurant a few minutes walk in Katamachi. No reservation needed, quick service, and reliably good. A practical lunch or early dinner option when everything else has a queue.

Miyabi no Yado Kanazawa
Kaga Kaiseki ¥¥¥

Restaurant in a converted samurai house on the edge of the district. The multi-course Kaga cuisine menu is built around seasonal local ingredients. The setting — low tables, tatami, shoji screens onto a small garden — matches the neighbourhood's atmosphere perfectly. Advance reservation required.

🗺

This neighbourhood is best explored on foot. Allow 1–2 hours — 15 minutes for the dobei lane circuit, 45 minutes inside the Nomura House, and 20 minutes for the water channel walk. The district is compact but rewards a slow pace: look up at the wall construction, listen for the water channels, notice how the lanes bend to prevent a clear sight line from end to end (a deliberate defensive feature).
