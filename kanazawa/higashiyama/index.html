About Higashiyama

Higashiyama (東山) sits on the east bank of the Asanogawa river, a 20-minute walk from Kanazawa Castle. It contains the Higashi Chaya district — the largest and best-preserved of Kanazawa's three geisha quarters — as well as the Higashiyama hillside trail connecting 70 Buddhist temples and Shinto shrines in less than 2 kilometres.

The Higashi Chaya district was established in 1820 by the Maeda clan, who concentrated the city's geisha in designated districts to make them easier to administer. The teahouses (ochaya) were built to a specific formula: latticed wooden facades, tatami rooms upstairs for banquets, secret passages between buildings. Several are still operating ozashiki geisha banquets today.

Above the chaya streets, the hillside climbs steeply through a sequence of moss-covered temple precincts — Utatsuyama, Kenroku Koen and the hillside shrines — where the only sounds are wind and occasional temple bells. It is one of the most peaceful walks in Japan.

Top Things to Do in Higashiyama

Free to walk

1 Walk the Higashi Chaya Streets

The main street (Higashiyama 1-chome) is the most intact Edo-period streetscape in Kanazawa. The latticed facades (koshi) and earthen walls are best in early morning light, before the tour groups arrive. Several teahouses have been converted to cafes and craft shops selling gold leaf goods — but the architecture is the attraction. Allow 30 to 45 minutes to simply walk and look.

¥700 · Open daily 9am–5pm

2 Shima Teahouse Museum

The most complete surviving ochaya interior in Japan. Built in 1820, it has been preserved exactly as it was in the Meiji period — tatami rooms, alcoves, lacquerware, shamisen instruments. The garden is tiny but perfect. It's the single best way to understand how an ozashiki geisha banquet would have worked: the rooms, the sound design, the sight lines from street to interior. Open daily 9am–5pm.

Free trail · Allow 90 min

3 Higashiyama Temple Trail

The 2km hillside path connects over 70 temples and shrines. No entrance fees for the trail itself; some temple gardens charge ¥100–300. Allow 90 minutes at a gentle pace. The path begins just above the Higashi Chaya streets and climbs through moss-covered stone steps and cedar groves before emerging at Utatsuyama Park with wide views across the city rooftops and, on clear days, to the Sea of Japan. Largely tourist-free even in peak season.

Free entry · Tastings from ¥300

4 Higashiyama Sake Breweries

Several sake breweries cluster along the river side of the district. Fukumitsuya — the oldest sake brewery in Kanazawa, established 1625 — has a tasting shop open to visitors with free samples and an excellent selection to buy. Hakuichi, adjacent, combines gold leaf craft with a sake and sweets shop. The breweries are best visited in the afternoon, after the morning crowds have moved on from the chaya streets.

Free · Spectacular April cherry blossom

5 Asanogawa Riverside Walk

The small river between Higashiyama and the city centre is lined with cherry trees (spectacular in April) and traditional machiya townhouses. The Tentomachi shopping street runs parallel. The 15-minute walk from the chaya streets back toward the city centre along the riverbank is one of Kanazawa's most pleasant transitions between neighbourhoods — a gentle decompression after the temple hillside.

Getting to Higashiyama

🚌

Loop Bus

Hashiba-cho stop (Line R) — 15 minutes from Kanazawa Station. ¥200 single, or ¥600 day pass. Runs every 15–20 minutes.

🚶

On Foot from Station

25-minute walk from Kanazawa Station via the Asanogawa riverside path — pleasant and flat. Well-signposted in English.

🏯

From Kenroku-en

20-minute walk east from Kenroku-en. Cross the Asanogawa bridge and the chaya streets begin immediately. No bus needed if combining with a castle visit.

🚕

Taxi

¥700–900 from Kanazawa Station. Approximately 7 minutes. Taxis readily available at the station's east exit rank.

Timing tip: Arrive before 9am for the chaya streets in near-solitude. The first Loop Bus from the station departs around 8:30am. By 10am tour groups begin arriving from Kenroku-en.

Where to Eat & Drink in Higashiyama

Kappou Itaru
Kaga Kappo ¥¥¥

Counter-style kappo cuisine using seasonal Kaga ingredients — local vegetables, river fish, and wild mountain produce. One of the district's most respected dinner spots. Reservations recommended for evenings.

Higashi Chaya Café Shizuya
Matcha & Wagashi ¥

Matcha and traditional wagashi sweets inside a restored teahouse on the main chaya street. The best place to pause between the sights and absorb the atmosphere. Seasonal wagashi change monthly.

Sushi Yoshida
Edomae Sushi ¥¥¥

Seven-seat counter near the Asanogawa bridge. The chef sources daily from Omicho Market — nodoguro, sweet shrimp, and seasonal shellfish. Walk-in at lunch, reservation required for dinner.

Kinjohro
Kaga Ryori ¥¥¥¥

Kanazawa's most celebrated Kaga ryori restaurant, a short walk from Higashiyama. The multi-course kaiseki menu draws on centuries of Maeda-era court cuisine. Reservation required — book several weeks in advance.

🗺

This neighbourhood is best explored on foot. Allow 2–3 hours — 45 minutes for the chaya streets, 90 minutes for the temple trail, and time to linger at the sake breweries. The flat riverside walk back to the city centre adds a pleasant 20 minutes.
