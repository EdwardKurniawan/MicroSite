Culture & History in Kanazawa

Two geisha districts still operating. A samurai quarter with original earthen walls. A castle rebuilt to exact historical specifications. A ninja temple. All within 20 minutes' walk of each other.

📍 Kanazawa, Japan ✍️ By Yuki Tanaka 🕐 Updated March 2026 ⏱ 9 min read

Kanazawa's extraordinary cultural survival comes down to two decisions made centuries apart. The first was the Maeda clan's, who ruled this domain from 1583 to 1871 with a single strategic priority: never give the Tokugawa shogunate a reason to attack. They achieved this by channelling their wealth not into military power but into arts, craftsmanship, and architecture. The result was a city of geisha districts, samurai residences, and ceremonial gardens rivalling Kyoto in quality — with far fewer tourists.

The second decision was made by Allied commanders in World War Two. Kanazawa had no significant military industry and was never a strategic target. While Hiroshima, Nagasaki, Tokyo, and Osaka were destroyed, Kanazawa's Edo-period streetscapes survived intact. The earthen walls of Nagamachi, the latticed teahouse facades of Higashi Chaya, the stone lanterns of Kenroku-en — all stand exactly as they were built.

Walking between these districts is not a reconstruction experience. It is the real thing. That is what makes Kanazawa unusual even within Japan, and why anyone who dismisses it as "Little Kyoto" has fundamentally misunderstood it. Kyoto is a rebuilt version of what it once was. Kanazawa simply never needed rebuilding.

The Four Essential Cultural Sites

🏮
Geisha District

Higashi Chaya District

The best-preserved geisha district outside Kyoto, and by most accounts more atmospheric than its Kyoto equivalents. The streets of latticed ochaya (teahouses) date from 1820, when the Maeda government consolidated the city's geisha entertainment into one regulated district. Several teahouses remain working ozashiki banquet venues where geisha still perform for private guests — the tradition is unbroken.

For day visitors, the Shima teahouse museum (¥700) offers an extraordinary glimpse inside a functioning ochaya: the tatami reception rooms, the painted folding screens, the musical instruments and lacquered banquet sets laid out as if a performance were about to begin. Arrive before 10am to have the streets largely to yourself.

Samurai District

Nagamachi Samurai District

The earthen walls (dobei) of Nagamachi are the most visible reminder that Kanazawa was once home to thousands of samurai retainers serving the Maeda lords. The walls — built from mud, straw, and roof tiles — run the length of narrow stone-paved lanes, intact from the Edo period. Behind them, several samurai residences survive, their garden gates visible through wooden fences.

The Nomura Samurai House (¥550) has the finest interior in the district: a collection of armour, swords, and clan documents, but more importantly a small rear garden considered one of the best townhouse gardens in Japan. The arrangement of stones, moss, and koi pond in a space barely 15 metres square is a masterclass in Japanese landscape compression.

Historic Temple · Booking Required

Myoryuji — The Ninja Temple

The name is a misnomer — no ninja ever lived here. Myoryuji was built in 1643 as a private temple for the Maeda clan, but its architect gave it a deliberate labyrinthine structure: 29 staircases, 23 rooms, hidden tunnels, false floors, a secret prayer room, a fake well (actually a 25-metre escape shaft), and enough concealed exits to allow the Maeda lords to flee in any direction if the castle fell.

Entry is by one-hour guided tour only, conducted in Japanese with translated handouts. The tour winds through increasingly disorienting spaces, each revealing another feature that looks like one thing and is actually another. It is architecturally extraordinary, historically fascinating, and frankly unlike anything else open to visitors in Japan.

Contemporary Art Museum

21st Century Museum

The circular building designed by Kazuyo Sejima and Ryue Nishizawa of SANAA is itself a landmark — a perfectly round glass-and-steel structure with no main entrance, designed so every direction offers an equal welcome. Inside, the free zone (no ticket required) contains several permanent installations including Leandro Erlich's Swimming Pool — a glass-floored pool through which visitors look down at other visitors standing "underwater." It is the most photographed work of art in Japan after Mount Fuji.

The paid exhibition zones (¥360–2,500) rotate regularly and are consistently world-class. The museum shop is excellent for design books and objects. The adjacent Kenroku-en means you can combine both in a half-day.

🏯 Kanazawa Castle

The castle of the Maeda clan was the second-largest castle complex in Japan after Edo (Tokyo). The main keep burned down in 1881 and was never rebuilt — but three major reconstructions between 1996 and 2020 have restored the Hishi Yagura turret, the Hashizumemon-tsuzuki gate complex, and the Kahokumon gate to exact historical specifications, using the same techniques and materials as the originals.

The result is the finest example of Edo-period castle reconstruction in Japan. The white lead-coated roof tiles (namari-gawara) are unique to Kanazawa — their silver-grey shimmer in rain or overcast light is one of the most distinctive architectural sights in the country. The Gyokusen-inmaru Garden, restored in 2015 on the castle's inner grounds, is a lesser-known gem that most visitors miss entirely.

🗼
Hishi Yagura Turret
The diamond-shaped turret is the castle's most photogenic structure. The paid interior (¥320) shows traditional timber joinery techniques. Combined ticket with Kenroku-en available.
⛩
Kahokumon Gate
The north gate, reconstructed in 2001, demonstrates the scale of the original defensive architecture. The stone ishigaki walls rising behind it date from the 17th century.

A daimyo garden hidden within the castle grounds — smaller and less visited than Kenroku-en but arguably more refined. ¥320. Open same hours as castle park.

📅 Full Day Itinerary
The Higashi Chaya → Kanazawa Castle → Kenroku-en → Nagamachi → 21st Century Museum walk can be done in a full day. Start at Higashi Chaya at 9am before tour groups arrive. Walk west to the castle (25 min), spend 45 min in the grounds and Gyokusen-inmaru garden, then cross the moat directly into Kenroku-en. Lunch near the garden south gate, then walk down to Nagamachi (20 min). End at the 21st Century Museum around 4–5pm and stay for the Swimming Pool in the early evening when it's quieter.

Kanazawa's Craft Heritage

The Maeda lords actively sponsored craftspeople — bringing the finest artisans from Kyoto and funding entirely new craft traditions. Four centuries later, Kanazawa remains one of the most concentrated craft centres in Japan.

🌸

Kaga Yuzen Silk Dyeing

Kanazawa's most refined textile art — hand-painted silk using a resist-dyeing technique that creates paintings of extraordinary detail directly on fabric. Unlike Kyoto Yuzen, which favours abstract patterns, Kaga Yuzen depicts naturalistic flowers, insects, and birds with botanical precision. Workshops in the Higashi Chaya area offer demonstrations and short lessons. Finished pieces sell from ¥50,000 upwards for scarves and kimono panels.

✨

Gold Leaf (Kanazawa Haku)

Kanazawa produces 99% of Japan's gold leaf — a near-monopoly that dates from Edo period restrictions on gold-leaf production in most other domains. The Higashi Chaya district and Nishi Chaya area are full of shops selling gold leaf applied to lacquerware, confectionery, sake, cosmetics, and ice cream. The Hakuichi workshop near Higashi Chaya offers live demonstrations of gold leaf beating, where a 5g block of gold is hammered into a sheet covering 6.6 square metres.

🏺

Wajima Lacquerware

Wajima-nuri is Japan's most prestigious lacquerware tradition, produced in the town of Wajima on the Noto Peninsula (90 minutes from Kanazawa). The technique involves over 100 separate applications of lacquer and powdered stone, producing a surface of extraordinary depth and durability. The Wajima Lacquerware Museum in Kanazawa city and the workshops on the Noto Peninsula are the best places to understand what distinguishes Wajima work from ordinary lacquer.

🪡

Kaga Embroidery (Kaga Nui)

One of Japan's three great embroidery traditions, practised in Kanazawa for over 400 years. Kaga Nui uses a distinctive thread-threading technique to create three-dimensional textures on fabric — the needle work can depict anything from chrysanthemums to crashing waves with an almost sculptural quality. Several small studios near the Nagamachi district accept visitors for observation by appointment. Finished pieces start from ¥20,000 for small panels.

🏮 Nishi Chaya — The Quiet Geisha District

Kanazawa's third geisha district (after Higashi Chaya and Kazuemachi) sits at the edge of the Teramachi temple district on the city's west side — a 10-minute walk from Myoryuji. It is smaller, quieter, and less visited than Higashi Chaya, which is precisely its appeal.

A free museum occupies one of the restored ochaya buildings, explaining the history of Nishi Chaya's geisha culture and displaying period costumes, instruments, and teahouse furniture. Unlike Higashi Chaya's Shima teahouse, this one charges no entry fee.

The district's relative obscurity means the surrounding streets retain a residential character — you may pass locals going about their day between the teahouse facades. The combination with Myoryuji makes Nishi Chaya an easy half-morning excursion from the city centre.

🎫 Free entry 🕐 9am–5pm daily 📍 Near Myoryuji
