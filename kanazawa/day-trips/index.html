Day Trips from Kanazawa

Kanazawa is positioned perfectly for three completely different day trips — a UNESCO mountain village, a wild peninsula coastline, and one of Japan's most celebrated hot spring regions.

Shirakawa-go 🏔️
UNESCO Heritage

Shirakawa-go — UNESCO Mountain Village

A remote mountain valley in Gifu Prefecture, 1h15min by direct bus from Kanazawa Station. The gassho-zukuri ("hands in prayer") farmhouses — their steep thatched roofs angled to shed the valley's extraordinary snowfall — are among the most photogenic structures in Japan. In winter (December–February), when snow blankets the rooftops and the valley is shrouded in mountain mist, the scene looks like nothing else on Earth. For this reason Shirakawa-go is often described as the most photographed landscape in Japan.

The village is small enough to walk completely in 2–3 hours. Most houses are still privately occupied; several open their ground floors to visitors. The Wada House (¥300) is the largest and most elaborately furnished. The Shiroyama viewpoint above the village offers the classic aerial photograph — a 15-minute walk up a steep path behind the main shrine.

Getting there
Nohi Bus from Kanazawa Station · 1h 15min direct
Bus cost
¥1,850 one way · ¥3,500 return
JR Pass
Covered with seat reservation

🎯 Top Things to Do

  • Shiroyama Viewpoint — The elevated view over the whole village. Best at dusk in winter when illuminations begin (December–March weekends only, timed entry required).
  • Wada House (¥300) — The largest gassho farmhouse open to visitors, with three floors of living spaces, original farm equipment, and a ceiling blackened by centuries of hearth smoke.
  • Myozenji Temple — Free to enter. The temple storehouse next door (¥300) is a quiet museum of traditional village tools and textiles.
  • Shirakawa Hachiman Shrine — The village shrine at the centre of the settlement. Free. The surrounding grounds are the main gathering point for the Doburoku festival in October.
  • Stay overnight — Day-trip crowds dissipate completely after the last buses leave at 5pm. If your schedule allows, stay in a minshuku (farmhouse guesthouse) for ¥10,000–15,000 per person including dinner and breakfast.

Noto Peninsula 🌊
Wild Coast

Noto Peninsula — Wild Coastline & Lacquerware

The Noto Peninsula juts 100km into the Sea of Japan north of Kanazawa — one of Japan's most dramatically rugged coastlines, with cliff-top roads, fishing villages, cedar forests, and the thousand-year-old Wajima lacquerware tradition. It is an entirely different landscape from Kanazawa city: remote, unhurried, and largely tourist-free. The 2024 earthquake caused significant damage in some areas; check current conditions at the Ishikawa tourism office before visiting.

Public transport on the peninsula is limited and slow — the most rewarding way to explore is by rental car, which gives access to the clifftop Noto Satoyama Road and the ability to stop at viewpoints, fishing ports, and the Senmaida rice paddies at your own pace.

Getting there
Car rental 90min · Limited train to Wajima
Recommended
Rent car at Kanazawa Station
Best season
May–Oct · Autumn foliage beautiful

🎯 Top Things to Do

  • Wajima Morning Market (朝市) — One of Japan's three great morning markets, running daily 8am–noon along Asaichi-dori. Fresh produce, dried seafood, lacquerware, and pickled vegetables sold directly by local farmers and fisherwomen.
  • Senmaida Rice Paddies — Over 1,000 terraced paddies descending to the sea on the Oku-Noto coast. Spectacular in late May when flooded and reflecting the sky; dramatic at harvest in September.
  • Noto Kiriko Festival Lanterns — The Oku-Noto Kiriko Festival (August) features enormous 15-metre illuminated lantern floats carried through fishing villages at night. Book accommodation many months ahead.
  • Wajima Lacquerware — Workshop visits can be arranged through the Wajima Lacquerware Hall (¥310). The finest pieces take six months to produce and last hundreds of years. Prices for a single lacquered bowl start at ¥30,000.
  • Stay in a minshuku — A family guesthouse on the peninsula includes dinner (fresh seafood caught that morning) and breakfast for ¥8,000–12,000 per person. The best value accommodation experience in Japan.

Kaga Onsen ♨️
Hot Springs

Kaga Onsen — Japan's Hot Spring Heartland

Four hot spring resort towns clustered in the hills south of Kanazawa, collectively known as Kaga Onsen: Yamanaka, Yamashiro, Katayamazu, and Awazu. Each has its own character and spring chemistry. All are 40 minutes from Kanazawa by JR Hokuriku Line — the easiest day trip on this list, and one of the most rewarding in Japan for anyone who has never experienced a traditional onsen ryokan.

The region's spring water has been attracting pilgrims and lords since the 8th century. Today, most major hotels and ryokan offer day-visitor bath access (higatari nyuyoku), allowing you to use their facilities without staying overnight — ideal for a day trip.

Getting there
JR Hokuriku Line · 40min from Kanazawa
Train cost
¥1,200 one way · JR Pass valid
Day bath access
¥500–1,500 at most hotels

🎯 Town by Town

  • Yamanaka Onsen — The most charming of the four towns. A mountain river gorge (Kakusenkei), traditional crafts street, and several excellent ryokan make this the first recommendation for visitors. The shared Soyu public bath (¥460) sits above the gorge.
  • Yamashiro Onsen — Slightly larger, with a more resort character. The Yunosaza shared bath is architecturally beautiful. Good base for combining with Daishoji temple town (10-minute bus).
  • Katayamazu Onsen — On the shore of Lake Shibayama. Quieter and less visited than the other three. Best for birdwatching and off-season travel.
  • Awazu Onsen — Japan's oldest recorded onsen, in operation since 717AD. The Zenshoji inn has been operating continuously since 1188 — the world's oldest hotel. Day visitors welcome at the shared public bath.
  • Daishoji Temple Town — Combine Yamashiro with a walk through Daishoji's old merchant district: a preserved Edo-period commercial street with lacquer shops, sake breweries, and the fine Jizoin temple garden.

Fukui 🦕
Half Day

Fukui — Dinosaurs, Zen & Soba

Fukui Prefecture, one stop south on the Hokuriku Shinkansen, offers three genuinely world-class experiences in a half-day: a paleontology museum that rivals anything in Tokyo or Osaka, one of Japan's most atmospherically complete Zen monasteries, and a cold soba noodle tradition strong enough to attract food pilgrims from across the country.

A JR Pass makes Fukui essentially free to reach. The combination of Fukui Dinosaur Museum in the morning and Eiheiji Temple in the afternoon is one of the most satisfying days available as a Kanazawa side trip — two completely different kinds of extraordinary in a single day.

Getting there
Hokuriku Shinkansen · 50min from Kanazawa
Train cost
¥2,200 one way · JR Pass valid
Ideal for
Families, Zen enthusiasts, food lovers

🎯 Top Things to Do

  • Fukui Prefectural Dinosaur Museum — World-class paleontology, ranked among the top three dinosaur museums globally. Fukui has produced more dinosaur fossil discoveries than any other prefecture in Japan. The life-size animatronic displays are astonishing. Allow 2–3 hours. ¥1,000 adults.
  • Eiheiji Temple — Headquarters of the Soto Zen sect, founded in 1244 by the monk Dogen. Active monastery with over 200 resident monks. The corridored connecting buildings on the steep forested hillside create an atmosphere unlike any other temple in Japan. ¥700 adults; shuttle bus from Eiheiji-guchi Station 40min from Fukui.
  • Echizen Soba — Cold buckwheat noodles served with grated radish (daikon oroshi) and a sharp dipping sauce. The technique produces a particularly coarse, earthy soba texture unique to Fukui. Eat at one of the lunch restaurants in Fukui city or near Eiheiji Temple. Expect to pay ¥800–1,200 for a set.
  • Maruoka Castle — One of Japan's 12 surviving original castles (not reconstructed), within 30 minutes of Fukui city. The keep dates from 1576. ¥450 adults.
Half-day option: Dinosaur Museum only (morning bus from Fukui Station, back by 1pm) leaves the afternoon free for Eiheiji or returning to Kanazawa. The museum is too good to rush — if you have children, allocate a full day.

⚠️ Important Booking Tip
Shirakawa-go bus tickets sell out weeks in advance in winter. The Nohi Bus service is popular from November through February when snow transforms the village. Book at kanazawa-station.com or the Nohi Bus counter at Kanazawa Station East Exit as soon as you have confirmed travel dates. Return tickets also sell out — do not assume you can buy at the village.

Getting to Kanazawa Station

All day trips depart from Kanazawa Station. The station has excellent English signage, a tourist information centre, and direct bus connections to the city centre (15 minutes).

🚅

From Tokyo

Hokuriku Shinkansen (Kagayaki/Hakutaka) from Tokyo Station — the fastest service takes 2h 30min. Trains run frequently throughout the day. Reserve seats in advance at busy periods (Golden Week, Obon, New Year).

~¥14,000 one way · JR Pass valid
🚆

From Osaka / Kyoto

Limited Express Thunderbird from Osaka or Kyoto to Kanazawa — 2h 15min from Osaka, 2h from Kyoto. Change at Tsuruga if travelling from Kyoto via the new Hokuriku section. Frequent daily departures.

~¥7,500 from Osaka · JR Pass valid
🏙

Station to City Centre

Kanazawa Station is 1.5km from the main sights. The Kanazawa Loop Bus (¥200 per ride or ¥600 day pass) connects the station to Kenroku-en, Higashi Chaya, and 21st Century Museum. Taxis take 10 minutes and cost around ¥1,000.

¥200 per ride by loop bus
📍

Within Kanazawa

The central sights are within 2km of each other and walkable for most visitors. The loop bus supplements walking for outlying areas (Nishi Chaya, Myoryuji). Rental bicycles available at the station for ¥500/day — excellent for flat city centre areas.
